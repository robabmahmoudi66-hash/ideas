Insect Bioconversion for Organic Waste Management in Iran
Closing the Waste Loop with Black Soldier Fly and Mealworm Farming
Author: Robab
Background: PhD Candidate, Canada (Insect Bioconversion / Waste Management)
Target Region: Iran — with learnings from Canadian and US industry
Focus Species: Black Soldier Fly (BSF) · Mealworms (Tenebrio molitor)
Key Outputs: organic waste management. High-protein animal feed · Organic fertilizer (frass
Abstract
Iran generates enormous volumes of organic waste — food scraps from households and markets, and residues from agriculture — most of which are either landfilled, burned, or left unmanaged. This represents both a serious environmental problem and a lost economic opportunity.
This project proposes building insect bioconversion infrastructure in Iran, using Black Soldier Fly (BSF) and mealworms to convert organic and agricultural waste streams into two high-value products: protein-rich insect meal for animal feed, and frass (insect excrement) as an organic fertilizer.
The model is already proven in Canada and the USA. The opportunity in Iran is to deploy it at scale where need — and potential impact — is greatest.
Why It's Interesting
Why Now
Iran's waste management infrastructure is under-resourced, and organic waste is a growing crisis in urban and agricultural regions.
Global demand for sustainable animal feed protein is surging — insect meal is increasingly recognized as a low-footprint alternative to fishmeal and soy.
The international insect farming industry (led by Canadian and US companies) has matured: the technology, regulatory frameworks, and business models are proven and transferable.
Why Iran
Iran has one of the highest rates of food waste in the region, with limited formal management systems.
Agricultural residues (crop waste, fruit processing by-products) are abundant and currently underutilized.
Strong domestic demand for affordable, locally-produced animal feed protein (poultry, aquaculture, livestock).
Organic fertilizer demand is growing alongside sustainable agriculture movements.
Limited competition in the insect farming sector — a first-mover advantage is available.
Expected Impact
Significant reduction in organic waste sent to landfill or burned.
New local supply of sustainable protein for Iranian animal agriculture.
Soil health improvement through organic frass fertilizer.
Job creation and entrepreneurial opportunity in agri-tech and the circular economy.
Approach
Phase 1 — Research & Feasibility (Year 1)
Map available waste streams: municipalities, food processors, farms.
Identify regulatory requirements in Iran for insect farming and insect-derived feed.
Review and adapt Canadian/US operational models for Iranian climate and infrastructure.
Identify potential pilot site partners (farms, food processors, municipalities).

Phase 2 — Pilot Operation (Year 2)
Establish a small-scale insect bioconversion facility (BSF first, mealworms secondary).
Run controlled trials with locally sourced organic and agricultural waste.
Measure key metrics: waste conversion rate, protein yield, frass quality, cost per kg of output.
Begin relationships with local feed and fertilizer buyers.

Phase 3 — Scale & Commercialization (Year 3+)
Expand capacity based on pilot learnings.
Develop supply agreements with waste generators (municipalities, farms, processors).
Commercialize insect meal and frass as branded products.
Build toward a replicable model for other Iranian cities and regions.

Closing Note
This idea is grounded in active PhD research on insect bioconversion conducted in Canada. The goal is to take proven North American models and deploy them in Iran — a country with the waste volume, the agricultural demand, and the economic incentive to make insect farming a transformative circular economy solution.
Insect bioconversion is not a futuristic concept. It is happening now, at scale, in Canada and the USA. Iran's challenge — and opportunity — is to bring this technology home.
